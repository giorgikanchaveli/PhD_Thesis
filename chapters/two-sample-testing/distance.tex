\section{Distances and Rademacher Complexity}\label{section:two_sample_distances}

In this chapter, we work with two distance functions on $\probprobx$ based on optimal transport: the Wasserstein over Wasserstein distance (WoW; see Definition~\ref{definition:wow}) and the Hierarchical Integral Probability Metric (HIPM; see Definition~\ref{definition:hipm}). For their topological properties and their relation to the Integral Probability Metric (IPM; see Definition~\ref{definition:ipm}), we refer the reader to Section~\ref{section:bnp_toolbox_distances}. In this section, we discuss the sample complexity of these distances, that is, the rate at which the estimation error of the empirical estimator decays with sample size, and its relevance to the threshold function developed in Section~\ref{section:rademacher_complexity}.

Let $\Y$ be a general Polish space and $\mathcal{H}$ be a class of measurable and bounded functions from $\Y$ to $\R$.

Given a class of functions $\mathcal{H}$, we define its Rademacher complexity
\begin{equation}
\label{equation:rademacher_complexity}
\mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}) = \sup_P \E \bigg( \sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \bigg| \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^n \epsilon_j h(Y_j) \bigg| \bigg),
\end{equation}
where the supremum is taken over all probability measures $P$ on $\Y$ with $Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \overset{\iid}{\sim} P$, and $\epsilon_1, \ldots, \epsilon_n$ are i.i.d.\ Rademacher random variables independent of the $Y_i$'s. For any probability distribution $P$ on $\Y$, letting $Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \overset{\iid}{\sim} P$ and $\hat{P}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{Y_i}$ denote the empirical estimator, we can control the random variable $\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P,\hat{P}_n)$. Following, for instance, the proof of Theorem 4.10 in \cite{wainwright2019high}, we have the following bias bound:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:IPM_R}
\E \, \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P,\hat{P}_n) \leq 2 \, \mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}^*),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}^* = \left\{h - h(y_0) : h \in \mathcal{H}, y_0 \in \Y \right\}$,
together with the following deviation bound: if all functions in $\mathcal{H}$ are bounded by $M$, then with probability $1-\theta$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:IPM_deviation_one_measure}
|\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P,\hat{P}_n) - \E \, \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P,\hat{P}_n)| \leq M \sqrt{\frac{2}{n} \log \frac{2}{\theta}}.
\end{equation}

As a consequence, for $P^1, P^2 \in \mathcal{P}(\Y)$, with probability $1-\theta$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:IPM_deviation_two_measures}
|\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^1_n,\hat{P}^2_n) -  \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^1, P^2)| \leq 4\,\mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}^*) + 2M\sqrt{\frac{2}{n} \log \frac{4}{\theta}}.
\end{equation}
Indeed, by the triangle inequality for IPMs,
\[
|\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{P}^1_n,\hat{P}^2_n) - \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^1,P^2)| \leq \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^1,\hat{P}^1_n) + \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^2,\hat{P}^2_n).
\]
Applying~\eqref{eq:IPM_deviation_one_measure} to each term with parameter $\theta/2$ and combining via a union bound, both hold simultaneously with probability $1-\theta$:
\[
\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^i,\hat{P}^i_n) \leq \E\,\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{H}}(P^i,\hat{P}^i_n) + M\sqrt{\frac{2}{n}\log\frac{4}{\theta}}, \quad i = 1, 2.
\]
Applying the bias bound~\eqref{eq:IPM_R} and summing yields~\eqref{eq:IPM_deviation_two_measures}.

Since concentration inequalities hold for any integral probability metric, and the distance functions we consider are special cases of it, we will show in Section~\ref{section:rademacher_complexity} that such inequalities can be obtained in our setting. 

Since the growth of the threshold is primarily governed by the Rademacher complexity of the function class, we now recall the known bounds on the Rademacher complexity for the WoW and HIPM.


The work \cite{catalano2024hierarchical} establishes an upper bound on the Rademacher complexity of the class $\mathrm{Lip}_1^*(\probx,\R)$. Namely, there exists $C>0$ such that, for $n$ large enough,
\begin{equation}
\label{equation:rademacher_wow}
\mathcal{R}_n(\mathrm{Lip}_1^*(\probx, \R)) \le C \frac{\log \log (n)}{\log(n)},
\end{equation}
which immediately entails a quantitative convergence result for empirical estimators in $\WW$ distance via~\eqref{eq:IPM_R}. Though it is unclear whether this bound is tight, the work \cite{catalano2024hierarchical} also provides a lower bound.
Specifically, let $\X$ be a bounded subset of $\R^d$ and $\widetilde{P}_1, \dots, \widetilde{P}_m \overset{\iid}{\sim} Q$. Then \citet[Theorem 4.1]{catalano2024hierarchical} shows that for $Q = \mathrm{DP}(\alpha,P_0)$, a Dirichlet process with absolutely continuous base measure $P_0$ and $\alpha > 0$, for every $\gamma>0$ there exists $c_\gamma>0$ such that, for $n$ large enough,
\[
\E \WW \bigg(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \delta_{\widetilde{P}_i}, Q \bigg) \ge c_{\gamma} n^{-\gamma}.
\]
That is, the convergence rate of the WoW is slower than any polynomial rate in $n$.

In addition, the work \cite{catalano2024hierarchical} provides bounds on the Rademacher complexity associated with the HIPM. In Appendix~\ref{appendix:reductionD}, we show that the HIPM is obtained as a special case of an integral probability metric with the function class
\[
\dstar = \{h:\probx\to \R: h(P) = g(P(f)), \ g\in \lipKzero, f\in \F^* \},
\]
where $K = \operatorname{diam}(\X)$.
Then, \citet[Theorem 4.3]{catalano2024hierarchical} shows that for the HIPM there exist constants $C_1, C_2$, and, for $d > 2$, $C_d > 0$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rademacher_hipm}
 \mathcal{R}_n(\dstar) \le 
\begin{cases}
C_1 n^{-1/2} & \text{if } d = 1, \\
C_2 n^{-1/2} \log(n) & \text{if } d = 2, \\
C_d n^{-1/d} & \text{if } d > 2.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
By~\eqref{eq:IPM_R}, it follows that the sample complexity of the HIPM is polynomial in $n$ and is unaffected by the infinite dimensionality of $\probx$. These results extend to the hierarchical empirical estimator $\widehat{Q}_{n,m}$ defined in Definition~\ref{definition:hier_emp_estimator}; see \citet[Theorem 4.4]{catalano2024hierarchical}.

With a distance function on $\probprobx$ in hand, the hypothesis testing problem takes the following form. Let $d$ denote either the WoW or the HIPM distance, and let $Q^1, Q^2 \in \probprobx$. We test the null hypothesis
\[
H_0 : d(Q^1, Q^2) = 0
\]
against the alternative
\[
H_1 : d(Q^1, Q^2) > 0.
\]
The test is based on independent samples
\[
\widehat{P}^1_{1,m}, \dots, \widehat{P}^1_{n,m} \overset{\iid}{\sim} \T_m[Q^1],
\qquad
\widehat{P}^2_{1,m}, \dots, \widehat{P}^2_{n,m} \overset{\iid}{\sim} \T_m[Q^2].
\]
The testing scheme we develop will reject $H_0$ if the distance between the hierarchical estimators exceeds some threshold. In the following sections, we discuss methods for determining this threshold.
